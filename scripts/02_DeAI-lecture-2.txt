What really helped me was when one of the Bittensor team members explained it with a specific school metaphor. Take a look: on the left we have the subnet owner — think of them as the principal of a school. The subnet owner sets the rules and the direction the subnet is going, defines the mission (what goal the subnet wants to achieve), and sets the performance criteria at a high level. On the right is the school building itself — that's the subnet as a place: the infrastructure and shared resources. It provides computational power and data; in other words, it's the platform where all the contributors interact. At the bottom left are the validators — think of them as the teachers. They come up with a task, send it to the students, then evaluate and grade the students' performance, and make sure no one is cheating. And the miners are the students themselves: they try to solve and crack the problems, competing for the best possible solution and the best possible grades. Students can sometimes cheat, so it's the job of the validator (the teacher) and the subnet owner (the principal) to prevent that. I hope that makes it clearer.

For subnets, there are already real-world use cases. I'd break them into three main categories. First, shared computation — people share computational resources that users can rent, for example a GPU or even a full virtual machine. We're using the Google Cloud Platform to run our virtual machine, but there are equivalent solutions on the decentralized network, and I think within the next year we'll be able to use them. The second big category is shared, decentralized AI intelligence — subnets trying to solve big problems. And the third category is subnets with a very specific goal.

Renting a GPU through Targon is cheaper than renting from AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud Platform — more than two, sometimes three times cheaper.
